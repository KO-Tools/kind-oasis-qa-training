Core Research Topics

Process Quality Assurance

▼

Process Quality Assurance focuses on ensuring that the processes used to produce cannabis products are effective, efficient, and consistently meet quality standards. It's a proactive approach that prevents defects rather than detecting them after they occur.

Key Implementation Areas:

  • Standard Operating Procedures (SOPs) development and maintenance
  • Process mapping and optimization for all production stages
  • Statistical Process Control (SPC) for real-time monitoring
  • Continuous improvement methodologies
  • Employee training on process standards

Cannabis-Specific Considerations: Due to the plant's natural variability and complex chemistry, process QA must account for batch-to-batch variations while maintaining consistent potency and safety standards.

Product Quality Assurance

▼

Product Quality Assurance ensures that finished cannabis products meet all specifications for safety, potency, and consistency before reaching consumers.

Essential Components:

  • Raw material inspection and testing
  • In-process quality checks at critical control points
  • Final product testing for potency, contaminants, and uniformity
  • Packaging integrity verification
  • Stability testing and shelf-life determination

Testing Requirements: Products must be tested for cannabinoid profiles, terpenes, pesticides, heavy metals, microbials, mycotoxins, and residual solvents according to state regulations.

Document Management

▼

Comprehensive document management is critical for maintaining compliance, ensuring traceability, and supporting continuous improvement in cannabis manufacturing.

Key Documentation Areas:

  • Batch production records with complete traceability
  • Equipment maintenance and calibration logs
  • Training records and competency assessments
  • Quality control test results and certificates of analysis
  • Deviation and CAPA documentation
  • Change control records

Best Practices: Implement electronic document management systems (EDMS) to ensure version control, accessibility, and compliance with data integrity requirements (21 CFR Part 11 for electronic records).

Risk Management

▼

Risk management in cannabis manufacturing involves identifying, assessing, and mitigating potential hazards that could affect product quality, safety, or compliance.

Risk Assessment Framework:

  • Hazard Analysis and Critical Control Points (HACCP) implementation
  • Failure Mode and Effects Analysis (FMEA) for equipment and processes
  • Contamination risk assessment (biological, chemical, physical)
  • Supply chain risk evaluation
  • Regulatory compliance risk monitoring

Cannabis-Specific Risks: Include cross-contamination between strains, potency variability, microbial growth during storage, and extraction solvent residues.

Nonconformance and Deviation Management

▼

Effective management of nonconformances and deviations is essential for maintaining product quality and regulatory compliance in cannabis manufacturing.

Key Processes:

  • Immediate identification and documentation of deviations
  • Risk assessment and impact evaluation
  • Root cause analysis using tools like 5 Whys or Fishbone diagrams
  • Disposition decisions (accept, reject, rework)
  • Preventive measures implementation

Distinction: Deviations occur during production (process variations), while nonconformances are identified in finished products. Not all deviations result in nonconforming products.

CAPA Management

▼

Corrective and Preventive Action (CAPA) management addresses systemic issues to prevent recurrence of quality problems.

CAPA Process Steps:

  • Problem identification from multiple sources (audits, complaints, deviations)
  • Investigation and root cause analysis
  • Action plan development with clear timelines
  • Implementation and effectiveness verification
  • Documentation and trend analysis

Important Note: Not every nonconformance requires a CAPA. Reserve CAPA for systemic or recurring issues to maintain system effectiveness.

GMP Fundamentals for Cannabis/Hemp

▼

Good Manufacturing Practices (GMP) ensure cannabis products are consistently produced and controlled according to quality standards appropriate for their intended use.

Core GMP Principles:

  • Personnel hygiene and training requirements
  • Facility design and environmental controls
  • Equipment qualification and maintenance
  • Raw material controls and supplier qualification
  • Production and process controls
  • Laboratory controls and testing

Regulatory Landscape: While federal GMP standards don't yet exist for cannabis, many states require compliance with food or dietary supplement GMPs (21 CFR 111 or 117).

Training & Competency Management

▼

Comprehensive training ensures all personnel have the knowledge and skills necessary to perform their duties in compliance with quality standards.

Training Program Elements:

  • Initial onboarding and GMP fundamentals
  • Job-specific technical training
  • Ongoing refresher training
  • Competency assessments and verification
  • Training effectiveness evaluation
  • Documentation and record keeping

Cannabis-Specific Training: Include state regulations, security protocols, seed-to-sale tracking, product handling, and contamination prevention specific to cannabis products.

Supplier Quality Management

▼

Ensuring the quality of raw materials and components through comprehensive supplier qualification and ongoing monitoring.

Supplier Management Process:

  • Supplier qualification and audit procedures
  • Quality agreements and specifications
  • Incoming material inspection and testing
  • Performance monitoring and scorecards
  • Continuous improvement partnerships

Critical Suppliers: Cannabis cultivators, extraction equipment vendors, packaging suppliers, testing laboratories, and ingredient suppliers for edibles.

Validation & Qualification

▼

Validation ensures processes consistently produce products meeting predetermined specifications, while qualification verifies equipment and systems are fit for intended use.

Validation Activities:

  • Equipment qualification (IQ/OQ/PQ)
  • Process validation for critical manufacturing steps
  • Cleaning validation for equipment
  • Analytical method validation
  • Computer system validation

Cannabis Applications: Extraction process validation, infusion homogeneity studies, packaging seal integrity, and environmental monitoring system validation.

Change Control & Internal Auditing

▼

Change control ensures all modifications to validated systems are evaluated for impact, while internal auditing verifies ongoing compliance and effectiveness.

Change Control Process:

  • Change request and impact assessment
  • Risk evaluation and approval workflow
  • Implementation and verification
  • Documentation and communication

Internal Audit Program:

  • Annual audit schedule covering all areas
  • Risk-based audit approach
  • Compliance verification against regulations and SOPs
  • Findings management and follow-up
  • Management review of audit results
